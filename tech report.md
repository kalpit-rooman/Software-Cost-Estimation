**Technical Summary**

The experimental results across the COCOMO81, Desharnais, and China datasets show a consistent pattern: classical machine learning models, particularly Random Forest and Linear Regression, achieve lower RMSE and MAE than CNN-based models, including CNN optimized with PSO. This outcome is technically expected because software cost estimation data is tabular, heterogeneous, and low-dimensional relative to image or signal data, whereas CNNs are designed around spatial inductive biases that do not naturally exist in such datasets.

A CNN is most effective when nearby input positions have meaningful local correlation and when the same local pattern can reappear at different locations. That assumption holds for images, audio, and time series, but not for software effort datasets where each column represents a distinct engineered attribute such as size, complexity, team capability, or project mode. In tabular data, the order of columns is usually arbitrary rather than semantic. A 1D convolution therefore imposes locality and weight sharing on features that are not spatial neighbors in any meaningful sense. As a result, the convolution filters may learn artifacts of column ordering rather than genuine project-effort relationships. Pooling operations can further remove useful signal because an isolated high-impact feature may be compressed together with unrelated neighboring columns. This architectural mismatch explains why CNN and CNN+PSO underperform despite tuning.

Tree-based models perform better because their inductive bias is much better aligned with software cost estimation. Random Forest and XGBoost learn decision boundaries by recursively partitioning the feature space, which allows them to capture threshold effects, nonlinear relationships, and higher-order feature interactions without assuming any spatial arrangement among inputs. This is important in effort estimation because project cost is rarely driven by a single smooth linear process; it often depends on conditional interactions such as “large project size combined with low team capability” or “high complexity combined with tight schedule.” Tree ensembles model these relationships directly. They are also robust to mixed feature types after encoding, insensitive to monotonic scaling, and generally less dependent on aggressive feature normalization than neural architectures. In practice, this makes them more stable on structured engineering datasets like COCOMO81, Desharnais, and China.

PSO plays a useful but limited role in this context. Its purpose is to optimize CNN hyperparameters such as filter count, kernel size, dense units, learning rate, dropout, batch size, or number of convolution layers. In the experiments, PSO improved the CNN relative to an untuned baseline, which indicates that the optimization procedure was working as a search strategy. However, hyperparameter optimization can only refine a model within the limits of its underlying architecture. If the model class itself is not well matched to the data structure, better hyperparameters may reduce the error margin but cannot remove the architectural mismatch. In other words, PSO improved training behavior and generalization slightly, but it could not transform a spatial model into a strong tabular learner. That is why CNN+PSO showed marginal gains over plain CNN yet still failed to surpass the strongest baseline models.

From a production perspective, these results justify selecting classical and ensemble models rather than CNN-based architectures for deployment. The goal in software cost estimation is not architectural novelty but reliable predictive performance, interpretability, reproducibility, and operational simplicity. Random Forest, XGBoost, Linear Regression, and their ensemble combinations satisfy these requirements better on the observed datasets. They produce stronger RMSE and MAE results, require less computational overhead than CNN+PSO, are easier to train and maintain, and are more appropriate for tabular industrial data. Therefore, for the final system, the technically justified choice is to prioritize classical machine learning and ensemble strategies for prediction, while presenting CNN+PSO as an experimental comparative approach that demonstrates the importance of matching model architecture to data characteristics.

**Viva-ready conclusion**

The key finding is that model suitability depends on data structure. CNNs are designed for spatially organized inputs, but software cost datasets are tabular and feature-driven, so their convolutional inductive bias is weak in this setting. Tree-based models outperform because they naturally capture nonlinear effects and feature interactions without relying on spatial locality. PSO improves CNN hyperparameters, but it cannot compensate for an architecture-data mismatch. Therefore, the most defensible production choice is to use classical or ensemble models, since they are more accurate, more stable, and better aligned with the nature of software cost estimation data.
